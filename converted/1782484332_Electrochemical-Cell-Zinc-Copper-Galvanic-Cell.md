Introduction
A galvanic cell is an electrochemical device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions. Also known as the Daniell Cell when using Zn-Cu electrodes with CuSO₄ solution

Historical Origin
Modern Applications
The concept was first demonstrated by John Frederic Daniell in 1836 through the invention of the voltaic pile
Galvanic cells are widely used in batteries, sensors, fuel cells, and corrosion protection systems across industries today.

Theory
In a galvanic cell, oxidation occurs at the anode and reduction occurs at the cathode. The reaction converts chemical energy into electrical energy by forcing electrons to move through an external circuit.

![preencoded.png](Image0.jpg)
Oxidation at Zinc Electrode (Anode)
01

Zinc loses electrons and forms zinc ions. This makes the zinc electrode gradually dissolve into the solution.
1. Electron release
Zinc atoms at the anode lose electrons and become Zn²⁺ ions.

![preencoded.png](Image1.jpg)
02
The released electrons leave the zinc electrode and travel through the wire.

2. Electron flow
Electrons move through the external wire from the zinc anode to the copper cathode, creating electric current.

![preencoded.png](Image2.jpg)
Reduction at Copper Electrode (Cathode)
Copper ions in solution gain electrons at the cathode and are converted into copper metal, which deposits on the electrode.
03

3. Ion balance

![preencoded.png](Image3.jpg)
The salt bridge allows ions to migrate between the half-cells so charge does not build up and the reaction can continue.
This gain of electrons is called reduction.

![preencoded.png](Image4.jpg)
04
Overall Reaction

4. Energy output
The two half-reactions combine to produce the net cell reaction.
As long as the circuit remains closed, the cell keeps producing voltage.

![preencoded.png](Image5.jpg)

The standard electromotive force (EMF) of the Zn-Cu galvanic cell is approximately +1.10 V. The salt bridge allows ion movement and maintains electrical neutrality in the cell. Without it, charge buildup would stop electron flow and the cell would quickly cease operating.

![preencoded.png](Image6.jpg)

### Notes:

<!-- Slide number: 6 -->
Role of Salt Bridge
The salt bridge is an essential component of the galvanic cell. Without it, the cell stops producing current after a short period.
Functions:
Connects the two half-cells and completes the circuit.
Allows ions to flow between the half-cells.
Prevents charge buildup that would stop the redox reaction.
Maintains electrical neutrality in the cell.
Ensures a continuous and stable flow of electrical current.

Without a salt bridge, the cell stops producing current after a short period.
